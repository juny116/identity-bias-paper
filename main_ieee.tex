\begin{abstract}
Large language models (LLMs) are increasingly used to critique and repair candidate solutions, often in a single prompt that asks for both a verdict and a corrected answer. We study whether this interface changes the verdict itself. In an initial two-stage diagnostic, verdict-plus-repair prompts increased false acceptance of wrong solutions by 9.0 percentage points for \texttt{gpt-oss-no-think} and 8.6 points for \texttt{qwen3-no-think}. A four-arm ablation shows that this apparent effect decomposes by critic. Gemma-4 shows a robust repair-request effect of +4.5 points (95\% CI [2.1, 7.2]) that remains positive when AIME is excluded. \texttt{gpt-oss-no-think} shows +4.6 points (95\% CI [0.0, 9.2]) overall, but the effect falls to +1.7 points (95\% CI [-3.0, 6.3]) without AIME. \texttt{qwen3-no-think} is null in both self- and cross-critique and is instead dominated by instruction and schema sensitivity. Field-order ablations show critic-dependent schema-order sensitivity: answer-first repair eliminates the \texttt{gpt-oss-no-think} inflation but amplifies the Gemma-4 inflation. Ordinary authorship labels are small relative to these interface effects. We recommend logging a neutral verdict before requesting repair.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Large language models are now routinely asked to evaluate other model outputs. They serve as automated judges in benchmark construction, graders in reasoning pipelines, critics in self-refinement systems, and repair agents in applications where an initial answer must be checked before it is returned to a user. In these settings, a critic is often prompted to perform two operations at once: decide whether a proposed solution is correct and, if it is not, produce a corrected answer. This coupling is convenient. It also creates a methodological risk. If the act of asking for a repair changes the verdict itself, then measured critique quality is partly a property of the interface rather than only a property of the model's reasoning ability.

Prior work on self-correction has mostly emphasized whether LLMs can improve their own answers without external feedback. The empirical picture is mixed and often negative: intrinsic self-correction is unreliable on reasoning tasks, can fail to detect errors, and can even degrade initially correct answers \citep{huang2024selfcorrection, kamoi2024selfcorrection}. Iterative self-refinement methods demonstrate that models can improve some outputs when prompted to critique and revise, but reasoning tasks remain difficult because the model must first identify a substantive error before revision is useful \citep{madaan2023selfrefine}. These results motivate a closer look at the first step of correction-oriented systems: the verdict.

Source labels are a plausible competing explanation for critique failure. Prior work on self-preference and self-correction blind spots suggests that a critic may treat a solution differently when it is attributed to itself, another model, a human, or an anonymous source. We therefore include identity-swap conditions in which the same solution is evaluated under different source labels while the content remains fixed. These conditions provide an important contrast: in this correction-oriented protocol, ordinary authorship labels produce small and unstable shifts, whereas prompt-interface changes produce larger and more diagnostic changes in false acceptance.

This negative identity result is informative because the same critics are not insensitive to prompting in general. A low-competence source label, \textsc{Weak Model}, consistently reduces false acceptance relative to anonymous framing, though the size varies by critic and dataset. The planned mirror condition, \textsc{Strong Model}, does not consistently move in the opposite direction: it is near null for Gemma and \texttt{gpt-oss-no-think}, and only mildly increases false acceptance for \texttt{qwen3-no-think}. The clean competence-axis interpretation therefore fails. What remains is a more practical pattern: source framing can modulate skepticism, but the dominant pathology is broad over-acceptance of wrong solutions.

The most consequential finding emerged when we separated first-stage verdicts from repair generation. In initial two-stage experiments, the critic first issued a verdict-only assessment and then, in an independent call, produced the standard verdict-plus-repair output. Holding critic, dataset, problem, solution, and identity condition fixed, the verdict-plus-repair interface accepted wrong solutions more often. Later ablations show why this result must be interpreted carefully: Gemma-4 provides the cleanest positive repair-request case in this study, the \texttt{gpt-oss-no-think} effect is concentrated in AIME, and the \texttt{qwen3-no-think} effect is better explained as instruction and schema sensitivity rather than repair coupling.

This manuscript studies that procedural effect as the central object. The paper's claim is not that repair requests are always harmful, nor that models should never produce corrected answers. The claim is narrower and more operational: verdict and repair are distinct stages, and bundling them can change measured verdict behavior for some critics, while other critics are more sensitive to instructions and schema details. For evaluation, logging, and reliability analysis, this matters. A system that appears to have a weak critic may partly be using a critic interface that encourages acceptance. Conversely, a system that separates verdict from repair can expose whether the failure lies in error detection, correction generation, or both.

The article makes three contributions. First, it provides a controlled empirical study of repair-request effects in LLM critique, including a four-arm prompt-interface ablation, leave-one-dataset-out checks, cross-critique replication, and a field-order mechanism test. Second, it places this effect in context by reporting identity and source-framing results across five critic configurations and four reasoning datasets, showing that ordinary authorship attribution is much smaller than expected in this correction-oriented setup. Third, it proposes and evaluates a simple gated critique protocol in which a verdict-only pass is logged before repair is requested, and compares it with a five-sample majority-voting baseline.

\section{Background and Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Self-Correction in LLMs}
\label{sec:rw-self-correction}

Self-correction asks a model to identify and fix errors in its own output, usually without external tools or oracle feedback. Early work on self-refinement showed that iterative generate--critique--revise loops can improve some generations, especially when feedback is explicit and the task rewards local revision \citep{madaan2023selfrefine}. Reasoning tasks are harder. A critique must identify a mathematical, logical, or factual error before a revision can help, and an unjustified revision may turn a correct answer into an incorrect one. Huang et al.\ report that large language models cannot reliably self-correct reasoning yet, and broader surveys reach similar conclusions about intrinsic self-correction without external verification \citep{huang2024selfcorrection, kamoi2024selfcorrection}.

Recent self-correction benchmarks sharpen this concern by distinguishing solution production from solution review. The self-correction blind spot describes cases where models fail to identify mistakes in outputs attributed to themselves but can detect similar errors in external outputs \citep{tsui2025blindspot}. This literature motivates our initial identity-swap design. However, our data suggest that in a correction-oriented JSON critique interface, the larger and more stable failure mode is not identity-specific blindness but over-acceptance of wrong solutions across source labels.

\subsection{LLM-as-Judge Calibration and Reliability}
\label{sec:rw-judge}

LLM-as-judge systems use models to evaluate candidate outputs, often replacing or augmenting human evaluation. Such judges can be useful, but their reliability depends on calibration, prompt design, and the match between the judgment task and the model's capabilities. Zheng et al.\ show that LLM judges can reproduce some human preferences in chat evaluation while remaining sensitive to position, verbosity, and presentation effects \citep{zheng2024judging}. Prior work already shows that LLM confidence can be miscalibrated and that elicitation strategy affects verbalized confidence \citep{tian2023justask}. Critique benchmarks similarly show that stronger models tend to be better critics, but critique quality remains far from solved on reasoning tasks \citep{lin2024criticbench}.

Our work does not claim overconfidence itself as a new phenomenon. Instead, we ask whether the same prompt-interface choices that change verdict behavior also change the calibration of the critic's reported confidence. A high false-accept rate means the critic approves incorrect reasoning and prevents downstream correction from even being attempted. This is distinct from correction quality conditional on rejection. A repair agent can only fix an error if the verdict stage first declares that the solution is flawed. The results therefore report verdict metrics separately from repair metrics whenever possible.

\subsection{Self-Preference and Authorship Attribution}
\label{sec:rw-self-preference}

Self-preference work shows that LLM evaluators can favor their own generations or generations attributed to their own identity. Panickssery et al.\ find that LLM evaluators recognize and favor their own outputs, while Wataoka et al.\ relate self-preference bias to distributional familiarity in LLM-as-judge settings \citep{panickssery2024selfpreference, wataoka2024selfpreference}. Lehr et al.\ further argue that assigned identity can be sufficient to induce strong preference shifts, indicating that the label in the prompt can matter even when the underlying content is unchanged \citep{lehr2025extremeselfpreference}. Xu et al.\ report that self-bias can be amplified during self-refinement \citep{xu2024pride}.

These findings provided the motivation for our identity conditions, but our empirical result differs from the strongest version of this prior expectation. In the present correction-oriented setup, ordinary identity labels produce small changes in false acceptance relative to the baseline over-acceptance rate. The difference does not imply that self-preference findings are wrong; it indicates that authorship attribution effects depend on the evaluation protocol, task, and output format. In our setting, interface design dominates identity framing.

\subsection{Prompt-Format and Structured-Output Sensitivity}
\label{sec:rw-prompt-format}

LLM behavior is sensitive to prompt wording and response format. Source framing experiments demonstrate that identical content can receive different evaluations when attributed to different sources \citep{germani2025sourceframing}. Structured-output prompts introduce another layer of sensitivity because the requested fields define what the model believes the task is. A JSON schema that asks only for a verdict may elicit a different policy from one that asks for a verdict plus a corrected answer, even if both prompts begin with the same instruction to review the solution carefully.

This is the key methodological concern addressed by the Caveat A ablation. The first two-stage diagnostic used a verdict-only prompt that explicitly stated not to produce a corrected answer. That instruction may itself have increased skepticism. The new ablation therefore separates three factors: the absence of a repair field, the explicit no-repair instruction, and the presence of a schema slot for a corrected answer.

\subsection{Critique-Repair Pipelines}
\label{sec:rw-pipelines}

Many self-improvement systems use a critique-repair pipeline: generate an answer, ask a model to critique it, and ask for a revision. Multi-agent debate and cross-model review methods extend this idea by using other models or agents to challenge an initial answer \citep{du2023debate, liang2023encouraging}. Cross-context review also suggests that separating production and review can improve output quality by reducing anchoring to the original generation process \citep{song2026crosscontext}.

Our contribution is narrower than multi-agent debate and simpler than model ensembles. We examine the interface inside a single critic call. A system can ask for a verdict first and a repair second, or it can ask for both at once. The distinction is small in implementation but large enough to change the measured false-accept rate. This makes critique-repair pipeline design a reliability issue, not merely a formatting preference.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{Task Setting}
\label{sec:task-setting}

Each experimental unit consists of a problem, a candidate solution, a ground-truth answer, a critic model, a source-framing condition, and a prompt-interface variant. The candidate solution includes the solver's reasoning trace and final answer. The critic receives the problem and solution and returns a structured assessment. The primary verdict outcome is whether the critic marks the solution as correct. For actually wrong solutions, marking the solution as correct is a false accept. For actually correct solutions, marking it as wrong is a false rejection.

The distinction between wrong and correct candidate solutions is determined before critique using dataset-specific answer checking. This design lets us measure the critic's verdict independently of whether its proposed repair is ultimately correct. When a repair is requested and supplied, we also evaluate whether the corrected answer matches the ground truth.

\subsection{Datasets}
\label{sec:datasets}

Experiments use four reasoning datasets that differ in domain and answer format. AIME contains competition mathematics problems with short numeric answers. GPQA contains graduate-level scientific multiple-choice questions designed to require expert knowledge \citep{rein2023gpqa}. MMLU-Pro is used as a broad multiple-choice reasoning benchmark. Olympiad contains challenging mathematical problems. Earlier exploratory runs included BBH, but it was dropped from the main analysis because it was too easy for the present critique setup and did not stress the verdict stage enough.

The existing full sweeps cover AIME, GPQA, MMLU-Pro, and Olympiad. The Caveat A prompt-interface ablation samples from these same datasets using seed 42: candidate identifiers are shuffled once, then the first 100 actually wrong solutions and first 50 actually correct solutions are selected per dataset, using all available items when a dataset has fewer than the target. The cached sample identifiers are stored under \texttt{logs/caveat\_a\_samples/}. This sampling preserves paired comparisons across prompt variants while keeping the ablation small enough to run quickly.

\subsection{Solver and Critic Models}
\label{sec:models}

The experiments include five critic configurations: \texttt{qwen3}, \texttt{qwen3-no-think}, \texttt{gpt-oss-think}, \texttt{gpt-oss-no-think}, and Gemma-4. The \texttt{qwen3} and \texttt{gpt-oss} variants include thinking and no-thinking configurations, which are analyzed separately because their critique behavior differs. The main two-stage verdict-repair diagnostic was completed for \texttt{qwen3-no-think} and \texttt{gpt-oss-no-think}. The four-arm Caveat A ablation was completed for \texttt{qwen3-no-think}, \texttt{gpt-oss-no-think}, and Gemma-4. Gemma-4 is used as a cross-critic evaluating \texttt{gpt-oss-think} solutions, while the two no-thinking critics evaluate their own model-family solution pools.

All main critics are served through the repository's model presets. \texttt{qwen3-no-think} uses \texttt{Qwen/Qwen3-32B} through vLLM with \texttt{enable\_thinking=False}; \texttt{gpt-oss-no-think} uses \texttt{openai/gpt-oss-20b} through vLLM with \texttt{reasoning\_effort=low}; Gemma-4 uses \texttt{google/gemma-4-31B-it} through vLLM. For all primary results in this paper (the Caveat A four-arm ablation, the field-order ablation, the gated mitigation pipeline, the N-of-K majority baseline, and the cross-critique replication), the critic experiment runners override the preset sampling parameters with \texttt{temperature=0.0} (deterministic decoding). The preset's default sampling parameters (\texttt{temperature=0.7} with \texttt{top\_p=0.8} and \texttt{top\_k=20} for \texttt{qwen3-no-think}; \texttt{temperature=0.7} for \texttt{gpt-oss-no-think}) were used only for some early legacy identity-swap sweeps; that data appears in this paper exclusively as legacy baseline context (Table~\ref{tab:baseline-far} and the broader source-framing summary), not in any prompt-interface contrast. Critic \texttt{max\_tokens} is set to 40000 for the local Qwen/gpt-oss critics and 12000 for Gemma-4. The default vLLM endpoint is \texttt{http://localhost:8000/v1}.

Candidate solutions are generated once and then reused across source-framing and prompt-interface conditions. This paired design ensures that changes in verdict behavior are attributable to the critic prompt or framing rather than to different underlying solution content.

\subsection{Source-Framing Conditions}
\label{sec:identity-conditions}

The source-framing experiment presents the same solution under six labels. The \textsc{Self} condition describes the solution as the critic's previous solution. The \textsc{Other Model} condition describes it as generated by another AI model. The \textsc{Anonymous} condition gives no source information. The \textsc{Human} condition attributes the solution to a human student. The \textsc{Weak Model} condition attributes it to a weaker, less capable AI model. The \textsc{Strong Model} condition attributes it to a stronger model.

The first four labels test ordinary authorship attribution. The weak and strong labels test whether source competence cues shift the critic's prior toward skepticism or deference. The pre-registered competence-axis prediction was that \textsc{Weak Model} would decrease false acceptance and \textsc{Strong Model} would increase false acceptance. Existing data support the first half but not the symmetric mirror pattern: \textsc{Weak Model} reduces false acceptance across critics, while \textsc{Strong Model} is near null for Gemma and \texttt{gpt-oss-no-think} and only mildly positive for \texttt{qwen3-no-think}.

\subsection{Prompt-Interface Variants}
\label{sec:prompt-variants}

The central procedural variable is the critic's output contract. The standard verdict-plus-repair prompt asks the critic to decide whether the solution is correct, describe the error if any, provide a corrected answer, and report confidence. The initial verdict-only diagnostic removed the corrected-answer field and instructed the model not to produce a correction before giving the verdict.

The Caveat A ablation expands this into four prompt-interface variants. The \textsc{Verdict-Only Instructed} variant is the original verdict-only prompt, including the explicit instruction not to produce a corrected answer. The \textsc{Verdict-Only Neutral} variant asks for the same three fields but removes the explicit no-repair instruction. The \textsc{Schema-Matched Verdict} variant uses the neutral verdict-only instructions but includes a fourth field, \texttt{corrected\_answer}, fixed to null, so that the response schema has the same number of fields as the repair prompt without requesting repair. The \textsc{Verdict+Repair} variant asks for the corrected final answer when the solution is judged wrong. Follow-up field-order ablations for \texttt{gpt-oss-no-think} and Gemma-4 add \textsc{Verdict+Repair Reversed}, which asks for \texttt{corrected\_answer} before \texttt{is\_correct}; this tests whether the verdict shift depends on schema-field order.

These four arms identify whether the observed repair-request inflation is caused by the repair task itself, by the earlier no-repair instruction, or by structured-output schema differences. The primary comparison is \textsc{Verdict+Repair} versus \textsc{Verdict-Only Neutral}. Secondary comparisons are \textsc{Verdict-Only Instructed} versus \textsc{Verdict-Only Neutral} and \textsc{Schema-Matched Verdict} versus \textsc{Verdict-Only Neutral}.

\subsection{Two-Stage and Gated Critique Designs}
\label{sec:two-stage}

The two-stage diagnostic issues independent calls for verdict-only and verdict-plus-repair outputs. The calls share the same problem, solution, and source preamble, but they do not share conversational context. This independence avoids anchoring the repair call on the first verdict and lets us compare how the output contract changes first-pass judgment.

The mitigation experiment uses a gated design. The critic first produces a neutral verdict-only assessment. If the verdict accepts the solution, the pipeline stops and records acceptance. If the verdict rejects the solution, a second repair prompt asks for a corrected answer. The gated design tests the practical recommendation implied by the main result: log a verdict before asking for repair, and request repair only after rejection. It is evaluated against the standard one-shot verdict-plus-repair pipeline on the same sampled items.

\subsection{Logging Schema and Answer Checking}
\label{sec:logging}

Each critique record stores the problem identifier, dataset, solver model, critic model, source-framing condition, prompt-interface variant, parsed verdict, error description, corrected answer when applicable, confidence, raw response, token counts, and the precomputed correctness of the candidate solution. The parser extracts JSON responses and falls back to conservative defaults when parsing fails; parse failures are logged for sensitivity analysis.

Ground-truth checking uses dataset-specific answer normalization. For short-answer mathematics datasets, the final answer is normalized before exact or symbolic comparison. For multiple-choice datasets, the selected option is compared against the answer key. Corrected answers are evaluated with the same checking function used for solver answers. The main verdict analyses do not depend on corrected-answer quality.

\subsection{Metrics}
\label{sec:metrics}

The primary metric is false-accept rate, defined on actually wrong candidate solutions as the fraction judged correct by the critic. Lower false-accept rate indicates better error detection. The corresponding metric on actually correct solutions is false-rejection rate, the fraction judged wrong by the critic. We also report detection accuracy over both correct and wrong solutions, correction success among rejected wrong solutions, harmful correction among correct solutions that are falsely rejected and then changed, and mean confidence. Calibration is reported as expected calibration error (ECE) using ten equal-width bins on stated confidence in $[0,1]$, computed per (critic, prompt-interface, dataset) and summarized both pooled and as an equal-dataset-weighted macro mean. Items that produced a parse failure at logging time are excluded from confidence-based metrics. As an end-to-end summary, we also report $P(\text{final correct}) = p \cdot (1 - \mathrm{HCR}) + (1-p) \cdot \mathrm{WRCR}$, where $p$ is the fraction of incoming solutions that are already correct, $\mathrm{HCR}$ is the harmful-correction rate on correct items, and $\mathrm{WRCR}$ is the repair-success rate on wrong items.

Repair metrics are interpreted separately from verdict metrics. A low false-accept rate is not sufficient if the critic rejects many correct solutions or generates poor repairs. Conversely, poor repair quality does not invalidate a verdict-stage finding; it indicates a different bottleneck in the critique-repair pipeline.

\subsection{Statistical Analysis}
\label{sec:stats}

All primary comparisons are item-paired. For headline false-accept differences, we report absolute percentage-point differences with paired bootstrap confidence intervals over problem identifiers. The primary analysis uses paired bootstrap intervals for the pre-specified prompt-interface contrasts. As a model-based robustness check, we fit per-critic generalized estimating equation (GEE) binomial logistic regressions with cluster-robust standard errors by problem identifier and \textsc{Verdict-Only Neutral} as the reference arm. We use GEE rather than a mixed-effects model in the final analysis because it gave stable cluster-robust estimates for every critic and prompt arm.

The primary pre-specified contrast is \textsc{Verdict+Repair} versus \textsc{Verdict-Only Neutral}. Secondary prompt contrasts examine the explicit no-repair instruction, the schema-matched control, the field-order arm, leave-one-dataset-out robustness, and cross-critique replication. Identity and source-framing contrasts use \textsc{Anonymous} as the reference condition and are treated as contextual analyses. We report both odds ratios and absolute percentage-point effects where model fits are used, with emphasis on absolute effects because the baseline false-accept rates are high. To control family-wise error on the per-critic Caveat A prompt contrasts (nine tests: three contrasts $\times$ three critics), we additionally report Holm--Bonferroni adjusted $p$-values estimated by paired bootstrap; results are summarized in Table~\ref{tab:holm}.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Over-Acceptance Is the Dominant Baseline Failure}
\label{sec:results-baseline}

Before considering source labels or repair requests, the critics already accept a large fraction of wrong solutions. In the legacy full-call sweeps, false-accept rates are 55.9\% for Gemma-4, 77.1\% for \texttt{gpt-oss-no-think}, and 56.9\% for \texttt{qwen3-no-think}. The neutral verdict-only ablation arms remain high: 46.7\%, 64.0\%, and 57.9\%, respectively. These values show that the main reliability problem is not a small identity gap but a broad failure to reject flawed reasoning.

\subsection{Verdict+Repair Increases False Acceptance in the Initial Two-Stage Diagnostic}
\label{sec:results-repair}

The initial two-stage diagnostic compares a verdict-only call against a verdict-plus-repair call on the same candidate solutions. For \texttt{gpt-oss-no-think}, false acceptance increases from 68.3\% under verdict-only to 77.3\% under verdict-plus-repair, a 9.0 percentage point increase (95\% CI [7.9, 10.2]). For \texttt{qwen3-no-think}, false acceptance increases from 47.4\% to 56.0\%, an 8.6 percentage point increase (95\% CI [7.6, 9.5]).

These results suggest that the standard correction-oriented interface changes verdict behavior. However, the original verdict-only prompt included an explicit instruction not to produce a corrected answer. The next subsection therefore reports the Caveat A ablation, which tests whether the effect survives under a neutral verdict-only prompt and a schema-matched control. As shown there, this initial result overstates the repair-request mechanism for \texttt{qwen3-no-think}.

\subsection{Caveat A Ablation: Separating Repair Request, No-Repair Instruction, and Schema Complexity}
\label{sec:results-caveat-a}

The four-arm prompt-interface ablation compares \textsc{Verdict-Only Instructed}, \textsc{Verdict-Only Neutral}, \textsc{Schema-Matched Verdict}, and \textsc{Verdict+Repair}. The primary contrast is \textsc{Verdict+Repair} minus \textsc{Verdict-Only Neutral}. If the effect is truly caused by the repair request, this contrast should remain positive while \textsc{Verdict-Only Neutral} and \textsc{Schema-Matched Verdict} remain close.

The frozen ablation results support a softened, critic-dependent headline. For Gemma-4, \textsc{Verdict+Repair} increases FAR by 4.5 percentage points relative to \textsc{Verdict-Only Neutral} (95\% CI [2.1, 7.2], $n=332$ paired wrong solutions). The GEE robustness model gives the same conclusion: log-odds coefficient 0.181, odds ratio 1.20, and $p<0.001$. For \texttt{gpt-oss-no-think}, the paired estimate is also +4.6 points (95\% CI [0.0, 9.2], $n=347$), but the model-based estimate is only borderline (coefficient 0.207, odds ratio 1.23, $p=0.055$). For \texttt{qwen3-no-think}, the repair contrast is 0.6 points (95\% CI [-2.5, 3.7], $n=356$), and the GEE estimate is null (coefficient 0.023, odds ratio 1.02, $p=0.715$). A two one-sided equivalence test further bounds the \texttt{qwen3-no-think} null: the 90\% CI is [-2.32, +0.84] points, equivalent under both $\pm$3 point and $\pm$5 point margins. The corresponding 90\% intervals for Gemma-4 ([+2.39, +6.64]) and \texttt{gpt-oss-no-think} ([+0.66, +8.56]) are not equivalent to zero.

The ablation also explains why the initial two-stage diagnostic looked stronger. The explicit no-repair instruction reduces FAR by 11.0 points for \texttt{qwen3-no-think}, by 3.2 points for \texttt{gpt-oss-no-think}, and by 1.2 points for Gemma-4. The GEE coefficients for \texttt{qwen3-no-think} are strongly negative for both the instructed arm (coefficient -0.484, $p<0.001$) and schema-matched arm (coefficient -0.239, $p<0.001$). Thus, for \texttt{qwen3-no-think}, the original Phase E inflation was almost entirely instruction- and schema-induced skepticism rather than repair-request inflation. We therefore interpret coupling verdict and repair as robust for Gemma-4, suggestive but dataset-dependent for \texttt{gpt-oss-no-think}, and unsupported for \texttt{qwen3-no-think}.

\subsection{Per-Dataset Heterogeneity}
\label{sec:results-heterogeneity}

The repair-request effect is concentrated in hard short-answer mathematics. Figure~\ref{fig:caveat-a-dataset} shows the per-dataset paired contrasts. AIME produces the largest positive shifts for Gemma-4 and \texttt{gpt-oss-no-think}, while GPQA and MMLU-Pro are near zero for Gemma-4 and individually null for \texttt{gpt-oss-no-think}. Leave-one-dataset-out analysis shows the key distinction: Gemma-4 remains positive without AIME, whereas \texttt{gpt-oss-no-think} falls to a null estimate. \texttt{qwen3-no-think} is heterogeneous across datasets but null overall.

Leave-one-dataset-out analysis sharpens the interpretation. Gemma-4 remains positive even when AIME is removed, falling from +4.5 points to +2.7 points (95\% CI [0.7, 5.0], $n=300$). In contrast, \texttt{gpt-oss-no-think} falls from +4.6 points to +1.7 points without AIME (95\% CI [-3.0, 6.3], $n=300$). The \texttt{gpt-oss-no-think} repair effect is therefore best described as AIME-driven rather than broadly stable across datasets. \texttt{qwen3-no-think} remains null under every leave-one-dataset-out exclusion.

The effect also depends on candidate-solution length. We stratify the two anonymous Caveat A arms (\textsc{Verdict-Only Neutral} and \textsc{Verdict+Repair}) into per-critic solution-length quartiles (characters in chain-of-thought plus final answer) and recompute the paired contrast. For Gemma-4, the repair-vs-neutral inflation is concentrated in the middle-to-upper range: $+15.5\,\mathrm{pp}$ ($[+6.9, +25.9]$) in Q3 (74k--105k characters) versus $\approx 0\,\mathrm{pp}$ at the shortest quartile (where the baseline FAR is already near the ceiling at 93\%). For \texttt{gpt-oss-no-think}, the largest contrast is in Q1--Q2 ($+6.5$ to $+8.1\,\mathrm{pp}$). For \texttt{qwen3-no-think}, only Q2 shows a positive contrast and the other quartiles are null or negative, consistent with the overall null. Per-quartile contrasts are tabulated in Appendix Table~\ref{tab:length-quartiles}. Length is therefore a modulating variable rather than a single threshold, and the repair-coupling effect is not a uniform shift over all input scales.

\subsection{Mechanism: Schema-Order Sensitivity Is Critic-Dependent}
\label{sec:results-field-order}

The field-order ablation tests whether the repair effect comes from merely including a repair field or from the order in which the model fills the schema. In the standard \textsc{Verdict+Repair} arm, the model emits \texttt{is\_correct} before generating \texttt{corrected\_answer}. In the reversed arm, the prompt asks for \texttt{corrected\_answer} before \texttt{is\_correct}. This schema-order manipulation matters for both tested critics, but in opposite directions.

For \texttt{gpt-oss-no-think}, the standard verdict-first repair arm raises FAR by 4.6 points relative to \textsc{Verdict-Only Neutral}, while the answer-first reversed contrast is only +0.3 points (95\% CI [-4.0, 4.3]), essentially identical to the schema-matched control. This is consistent with a lock-in account: when the verdict comes first, the model can accept before it has forced itself to construct an alternative answer; when correction comes first, the inflation disappears.

Gemma-4 rules out a universal generation-order mechanism. Its standard verdict-first repair arm raises FAR by 4.5 points, but its answer-first reversed arm raises FAR by 7.2 points relative to neutral, 2.7 points higher than the standard repair arm. The per-dataset pattern is also mixed: reversed reduces the AIME inflation but worsens GPQA and MMLU-Pro and leaves Olympiad unchanged relative to standard repair. For Gemma-4, putting \texttt{corrected\_answer} first may pressure the model to produce or accommodate a correction even when the error is not resolved, and then accept the wrong candidate alongside that generated answer. The defensible mechanism claim is therefore narrower: schema-field order is a real interface variable, but its direction is critic-specific.

\subsection{Source Framing Is Secondary and Asymmetric}
\label{sec:results-identity}

Across the five critic configurations, ordinary identity labels produce much smaller shifts than the repair interface. In the full Gemma sweep over four datasets and 725 wrong solutions per condition, false-accept rates were 0.559 for \textsc{Anonymous}, 0.564 for \textsc{Human}, 0.537 for \textsc{Other Model}, and 0.543 for \textsc{Self}. The range across these identity-only labels is 2.7 percentage points. The same qualitative clustering appears in \texttt{qwen3-no-think}, where identity-only false-accept rates fall around 0.46--0.49, and in \texttt{gpt-oss-no-think}, where they fall around 0.68--0.69.

This result does not refute self-preference effects in all LLM evaluation settings. It shows that, in the present correction-oriented protocol, ordinary authorship attribution is not the main driver of critique failure. The critics can respond to prompts, but their broad over-acceptance and repair-interface sensitivity are larger than the self-versus-other gap.

Low-competence framing is more consistent than ordinary authorship labeling, but it remains modest and asymmetric. The \textsc{Weak Model} condition lowers false acceptance relative to \textsc{Anonymous} for Gemma-4 and \texttt{qwen3-no-think}, with a smaller matched full-call effect for \texttt{gpt-oss-no-think}. The \textsc{Strong Model} falsifier rules out a clean competence axis: if critics were using expected source competence as a symmetric prior, strong-source framing should increase false acceptance by an amount comparable to the weak-source decrease. Instead, \textsc{Strong Model} is near null for Gemma-4 and \texttt{gpt-oss-no-think}, and only mildly positive for \texttt{qwen3-no-think}. We therefore describe low-competence framing as an asymmetric skepticism cue rather than evidence for a monotonic competence scale. Sparse strong-source rows for thinking variants are not interpreted because they were run on very small subsets.

\subsection{Gated Verdict-Then-Repair as a Minimal Mitigation}
\label{sec:results-mitigation}

The mitigation experiment compares the standard one-shot verdict-plus-repair pipeline against a gated pipeline that first logs a neutral verdict-only assessment and requests repair only after rejection. The goal is not to prove that the gated pipeline solves self-correction. The goal is to test whether separating verdict from repair reduces false acceptance without causing a compensating increase in false rejection or harmful correction.

The gated pipeline mirrors the Caveat A pattern. For Gemma-4, gating reduces wrong-solution acceptance by 4.5 percentage points in the anonymous condition (95\% CI [-6.9, -2.1]) and 3.6 points in the other-model condition (95\% CI [-6.0, -1.2]). For \texttt{gpt-oss-no-think}, it reduces wrong-solution acceptance by 4.6 points in anonymous, but the confidence interval touches zero (95\% CI [-9.2, 0.0]), and by 3.5 points in self (95\% CI [-7.8, +0.9]). For \texttt{qwen3-no-think}, where the repair-request contrast is null, the gated effect is correspondingly small and not significant: -0.6 points in anonymous and -1.4 points in self. Correct-solution acceptance changes little, and harmful correction does not systematically increase; for Gemma anonymous, $\Delta$TAR is -1.0 points (95\% CI [-3.5, +1.5]) and $\Delta$harmful correction is 0.0.

\subsection{Repair Quality Remains Poor After Correct Rejection}
\label{sec:results-repair-quality}

Separating verdict from repair is also necessary because repair quality is weak even when the critic correctly detects an error. Among wrong solutions that are correctly rejected, repair success is only 10--22\% for the main no-thinking and Gemma critics: Gemma-4 is roughly 17\% in the Caveat A repair arms, \texttt{gpt-oss-no-think} is roughly 17\%, and \texttt{qwen3-no-think} is roughly 10\%. The best legacy configuration, \texttt{gpt-oss-think}, reaches about 33\%, still leaving most detected errors unrepaired. The other side of the tradeoff is worse: among correct solutions that are falsely rejected, harmful-correction rates range from 53\% to 98\% across the main configurations, meaning that once a critic wrongly rejects a correct answer, it usually replaces it with an incorrect one.

The field-order ablation improves repair success as well as verdict behavior for \texttt{gpt-oss-no-think}, but with a safety tradeoff. In the standard verdict-first repair arm, macro repair success is 21.4\% across datasets; in the answer-first reversed arm, it rises to 25.5\%. However, macro harmful correction also rises from 75.0\% to 83.9\%, and Gemma-4's reversed arm reaches 100\% macro harmful correction among falsely rejected correct solutions. The practical recommendation is therefore not to deploy answer-first repair as a standalone fix. Reversed schema can reduce FAR for one critic, but it should be paired with a separate harmful-correction check and reported independently from verdict reliability.

The \texttt{qwen3-no-think} null therefore is not an artifact of self-critique; it holds in both self- and cross-critique designs.

\subsection{Prompt Interface Shifts Calibration}
\label{sec:results-calibration}

A natural fallback is to ask whether the confidence field identifies unreliable verdicts. It does not solve the interface problem, because calibration itself shifts with the prompt interface. Absolute LLM overconfidence is already well documented, so the calibration result here is not that critics are overconfident in general. The interface-specific result is that calibration changes when the verdict interface changes. Gemma-4's ECE moves from 0.258 in the legacy full-call prompt to 0.314 in the verdict-only neutral prompt and 0.290 in the verdict-plus-repair prompt. \texttt{gpt-oss-no-think} is consistently poorly calibrated, moving from 0.389 to 0.395 and 0.404. The largest shift is \texttt{qwen3-no-think}: ECE worsens from 0.275 in the legacy setting to 0.463 under the verdict-only neutral interface and remains high at 0.416 under verdict-plus-repair. Equal-dataset macro ECE shows the same pattern, moving from 0.263 to 0.460 and 0.419, so the shift is not a pooled-data weighting artifact. This supports the interface-effect thesis: prompt changes that alter false acceptance can also reshape the confidence attached to those verdicts.

To test whether a simple post-hoc fix can absorb this interface dependence, we fit a per-arm temperature-scaling parameter $T$ to the critic's stated verdict confidence by minimizing the binary negative log-likelihood on verdict correctness. The optimal $T$ saturates the upper bound of the search range ($T=10$) for every critic and arm, which means the raw confidences are extremely overconfident: scaling pushes them strongly toward $0.5$ regardless of interface. After scaling, the 10-bin ECE drops by 18--32 percentage points; residual ECE is 7--21\%, and the relative ordering across arms is preserved (the verdict-only and repair arms remain less well-calibrated than the legacy full sweep for \texttt{qwen3-no-think}). Per-interface temperature scaling therefore reduces but does not eliminate the interface-driven calibration shift, and the gated protocol's recommendation to log a verdict-only confidence is more robust than relying on a single post-hoc temperature.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Practical Implications}
\label{sec:discussion-practical}

The main measurement implication is simple: evaluation systems should not treat a verdict collected from a verdict-plus-repair prompt as interchangeable with a verdict collected from a verdict-only prompt. Gemma-4 is the cleanest positive repair-request case in this study, \texttt{gpt-oss-no-think} shows the same overall direction but is AIME-driven, and \texttt{qwen3-no-think} is dominated by instruction and schema sensitivity. All three cases support the same measurement recommendation: when the purpose is to measure critic reliability, detect errors, or estimate calibration, the verdict should be elicited and logged before any repair request. Repair can still be useful, but it should be downstream of a recorded judgment rather than fused with it.

This recommendation is especially important for self-correction pipelines. A coupled prompt may make the system appear worse at error detection than it would be under a clean verdict interface, or it may hide which stage is failing. If the critic accepts a wrong answer, the repair stage never has a chance. If the critic rejects correctly but repairs poorly, the bottleneck is different. A two-stage or gated design makes these failures visible.

The results also caution against over-interpreting authorship labels in correction-oriented critique. In this setup, self, other-model, anonymous, and human labels are small compared with the interface effect. Low-competence framing can increase skepticism, but it does not provide a general solution and does not behave like a symmetric competence scale. Prompting a critic to distrust weak sources is not the same as making it a reliable verifier. Confidence is not a stable escape hatch either: although LLM overconfidence is already well documented, the critique interface changes calibration itself, most sharply for \texttt{qwen3-no-think}, whose ECE worsens by 0.19 under the neutral verdict-only arm.

\subsection{Mechanism Speculation}
\label{sec:discussion-mechanism}

The field-order results support a critic-specific interface account rather than a single generation-order mechanism. A verdict-only prompt asks the model to decide whether the solution is correct. A standard verdict-plus-repair prompt asks it to emit the verdict first and then produce an assessment package in which rejecting the solution creates an additional obligation: explain the error and supply a corrected final answer. For \texttt{gpt-oss-no-think}, reversing the order appears to break a verdict-first lock-in: when the prompt asks for the corrected answer before the verdict, the repair-inflation effect disappears and repair success rises from roughly 17\% to roughly 25\%.

Gemma-4 does not follow that mechanism. Its answer-first arm amplifies false acceptance rather than reducing it. One plausible account is that answer-first repair creates a different pressure: Gemma may commit to a correction-like continuation before deciding whether the candidate is wrong, then accept the original wrong answer alongside that continuation. This interpretation is necessarily speculative, but the empirical point is clear. Schema-format choices matter, and they can matter differently for different critics.

Other mechanisms remain possible, and \texttt{qwen3-no-think} shows why they matter. For that critic, the explicit no-repair instruction lowers FAR by 11.0 percentage points relative to the neutral verdict-only prompt, and the schema-matched arm also lowers FAR. The original Phase E result for \texttt{qwen3-no-think} is therefore not evidence of repair-request inflation; it is evidence of strong prompt-format sensitivity. Token budget is a less compelling explanation by itself because the verdict fields are short and the observed changes are specifically about accepting wrong answers, but the broader lesson is that instruction wording, schema design, and repair coupling all need to be separated experimentally.

\subsection{Relationship to Identity-Bias Work}
\label{sec:discussion-identity}

The identity-swap results should be read as a scoped negative result. Prior work shows that LLMs can exhibit self-preference and source-framing biases in evaluation settings \citep{panickssery2024selfpreference, lehr2025extremeselfpreference, germani2025sourceframing}. Our data show that those effects do not dominate in this correction-oriented protocol. The same solution presented as self, anonymous, human, or another model produces only small shifts in false acceptance. This difference may arise because correction-oriented critique requires error detection rather than preference ranking, because the JSON schema constrains the response, or because the datasets expose a broad over-acceptance failure that swamps identity labels.

The broader lesson is that self-correction failure should not be attributed to a single cause. Identity, source competence, context, output schema, field order, and repair requests can all influence the critic. The relative importance and even the direction of these factors depends on the model and task interface. In our experiments, repair coupling is a robust source of over-acceptance for Gemma-4, an AIME-concentrated effect for \texttt{gpt-oss-no-think}, and not supported for \texttt{qwen3-no-think}, which is dominated by instruction and schema sensitivity.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

The first limitation is critic and dataset dependence. Gemma-4 is the clean positive case, including under leave-one-dataset-out analysis. \texttt{gpt-oss-no-think} is positive overall but becomes null when AIME is excluded, so its effect should be described as concentrated in hard short-answer mathematics rather than as a broad benchmark-wide effect. \texttt{qwen3-no-think} does not show repair inflation in either self- or cross-critique; its behavior is dominated by the no-repair instruction and schema-matched control. Thinking-enabled variants are represented more sparsely in the repair-interface experiments because of runtime cost.

Second, the results are prompt-specific. Even after the Caveat A and field-order ablations, the findings are tied to the prompt templates, JSON schemas, field order, and source preambles used here. Other templates may reduce or amplify the repair-request effect. This is not a reason to ignore the result; it is the reason the result matters. Interface choices that appear minor can change verdict behavior.

Third, the experiments focus on reasoning benchmarks with checkable answers. This makes false acceptance and correction success measurable, but it may not generalize to open-ended writing, summarization, or subjective preference evaluation. In open-ended domains, the boundary between verdict and repair may be less crisp, and human evaluation may be needed to assess whether a repair improved the output.

Fourth, the answer-checking functions are imperfect. Mathematical equivalence, multiple-choice extraction, and boxed-answer parsing can introduce noise. Because the core comparisons are paired across prompt variants, such noise should affect all arms similarly, but absolute rates should still be interpreted with this measurement layer in mind.

Fifth, parse failures are not negligible for full-call arms in the local no-thinking models. In the legacy full-call logs, the parse-failure proxy is 6.1\% for \texttt{gpt-oss-no-think} and 4.3\% for \texttt{qwen3-no-think}. In the Caveat A \textsc{Verdict+Repair} arm, the rates are 8.5\% and 5.5\%, respectively, while the other Caveat A arms are approximately 0\%. Gemma-4 is near 0\% throughout. The main verdict comparisons are still useful, but full-call structured output is itself less reliable for these critics.

Sixth, some legacy source-framing runs were collected across multiple log subdirectories and solver configurations. The main repair-interface ablation uses locked sample identifiers and paired prompt variants to avoid this issue. Legacy identity and framing results are used primarily as contextual evidence rather than as the sole basis for the headline claim.

Seventh, several design probes were not run in this revision and are appropriate follow-up work. A matched-schema two-shot protocol that issues fully independent verdict-only and verdict+repair calls with identical preambles would further isolate the mechanism beyond the within-call schema-matched control already reported in the Caveat A ablation. A larger-scale sensitivity analysis over candidate-solution length and structure would build on the per-quartile contrasts reported here. Replicating the Caveat A and field-order ablations on thinking-enabled critics (\texttt{qwen3-think}, \texttt{gpt-oss-think}) would test whether the mechanisms persist when the critic invests more reasoning per call; we did not run this in the current revision because of compute cost. A deterministic-decoding replication using sampling-mode critics is a related sensitivity check, although all primary Caveat A and field-order runs in this paper already use \texttt{temperature=0.0} (see Section~\ref{sec:models}).

Finally, lower false acceptance does not imply reliable correction. Even after a correct rejection of a wrong answer, repair success is low for the main critics, and when a critic falsely rejects a correct answer it often replaces it with a wrong one. For this reason, the paper reports false acceptance, false rejection, correction success, and harmful correction separately, and treats gated verdict logging as a measurement recommendation rather than a claim that the repair stage is reliable.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

LLM critique systems often bundle judgment and repair into a single prompt. This paper shows that the bundle can change the judgment, but not uniformly across critics or datasets. Gemma-4 provides the cleanest positive repair-request case, \texttt{gpt-oss-no-think} shows an AIME-concentrated effect, and \texttt{qwen3-no-think} is better explained by instruction and schema sensitivity than by repair coupling. Field-order results further show that schema order is a critic-dependent interface variable rather than a universal mechanism. In parallel, identity-swap experiments show that ordinary authorship labels are small relative to the over-acceptance baseline, while low-competence framing is a modest asymmetric skepticism cue. The practical recommendation is to separate verdict from repair when evaluating or deploying critique pipelines: a critic should first decide whether a solution is correct, and only after that verdict is logged should a repair be requested. This protocol distinction makes it possible to diagnose whether failure comes from accepting wrong answers, rejecting correct ones, confidence estimates that shift with the prompt interface, or generating poor fixes after a correct rejection.

\section{Prompt Templates}
\label{app:prompts}

All critic prompts use the same problem statement, candidate reasoning, and final answer. Source-framing conditions vary only the preamble. The \textsc{Self} preamble states that the solution is the critic's previous solution and asks it to review its own work. The \textsc{Other Model} preamble states that the solution was generated by another AI model. The \textsc{Anonymous} preamble gives no source attribution. The \textsc{Human} preamble states that the solution was written by a human student. The \textsc{Weak Model} preamble states that the solution was generated by a weaker, less capable AI model.
