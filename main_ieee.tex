\begin{abstract}
Large language models (LLMs) are increasingly used as critics that judge candidate solutions and, in the same structured response, propose repairs. This paper asks whether such a critic call is a stable measurement of the critic's verdict. We test source labels, repair requests, schema variants, field order, confidence reporting, and repair generation across reasoning benchmarks and critic families. The main result is not a universal effect of repair requests, but a broader measurement problem: prompt-interface perturbations move verdict and measurement outcomes more than ordinary identity labels. Parser-aware replay shows why this distinction matters: apparent verdict-plus-repair false-acceptance inflation can be dominated by malformed structured outputs combined with a permissive parser fallback. Repair quality and confidence reporting do not rescue the pipeline, since successful repairs remain uncommon, harmful correction remains possible, and confidence-based abstention can preserve or even concentrate high-confidence false accepts. We therefore recommend separating measurement from intervention: elicit and log a neutral verdict, including parse status, before requesting any repair, and report verdict accuracy, parse failures, repair success, harmful correction, and calibration as distinct failure modes.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Large language models are now routinely asked to evaluate other model outputs. They serve as automated judges in benchmark construction, graders in reasoning pipelines, critics in self-refinement systems, and repair agents in applications where an initial answer must be checked before it is returned to a user. In these settings, a critic is often prompted to perform two operations at once: decide whether a proposed solution is correct and, if it is not, produce a corrected answer. This coupling is convenient. It also creates a methodological risk. If the interface changes either the verdict or the reliability with which that verdict can be parsed, then measured critique quality is partly a property of the measurement channel rather than only a property of the model's reasoning ability.

Prior work on self-correction has mostly emphasized whether LLMs can improve their own answers without external feedback. The empirical picture is mixed and often negative: intrinsic self-correction is unreliable on reasoning tasks, can fail to detect errors, and can even degrade initially correct answers \citep{huang2024selfcorrection, kamoi2024selfcorrection}. Iterative self-refinement methods demonstrate that models can improve some outputs when prompted to critique and revise, but reasoning tasks remain difficult because the model must first identify a substantive error before revision is useful \citep{madaan2023selfrefine}. These results motivate a closer look at the first step of correction-oriented systems: the verdict.

Two explanations for critique failure are plausible. One is source bias: a critic may trust or distrust a solution depending on whether it is labeled as self-authored, human-authored, produced by another model, or produced by a weak or strong model. The other is interface brittleness: the critic's measured verdict may depend on the output contract, schema, field order, repair request, or parser behavior used to collect that verdict. This paper compares these explanations by holding solution content fixed while varying both source labels and critique interfaces.

The central finding is that interface variation is larger and more operationally consequential than ordinary identity variation. Across the critics for which both measurements are available, the range induced by prompt-interface arms is several times larger than the range induced by the four ordinary identity labels. The most important lesson is not that one particular repair prompt universally makes critics over-accept wrong answers. Parser-aware replay shows that the strongest apparent verdict-plus-repair effect is dominated by malformed structured outputs and a permissive fallback that maps parse failure to acceptance. The broader and more defensible claim is that a single structured critic call is a brittle measurement instrument unless verdict, parse status, confidence, and repair quality are logged separately.

The article makes four contributions. First, it quantifies the contrast between source-label effects and prompt-interface effects, showing that interface variation dominates ordinary identity-label variation in this correction-oriented setting. Second, it audits parser sensitivity in verdict-plus-repair prompts and shows that naive parsed-output contrasts can misstate behavioral verdict effects. Third, it reports additional interface perturbations, including instruction effects, schema-order sensitivity, calibration shifts, thinking-versus-no-thinking comparisons, and confidence-based abstention curves. Fourth, it evaluates repair quality and end-to-end pipeline utility, motivating a practical measurement protocol: elicit and log a neutral verdict with parse status before requesting any repair.

\section{Background and Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Self-Correction in LLMs}
\label{sec:rw-self-correction}

Self-correction asks a model to identify and fix errors in its own output, usually without external tools or oracle feedback. Early work on self-refinement showed that iterative generate--critique--revise loops can improve some generations, especially when feedback is explicit and the task rewards local revision \citep{madaan2023selfrefine}. Reasoning tasks are harder. A critique must identify a mathematical, logical, or factual error before a revision can help, and an unjustified revision may turn a correct answer into an incorrect one. Huang et al.\ report that large language models cannot reliably self-correct reasoning yet, and broader surveys reach similar conclusions about intrinsic self-correction without external verification \citep{huang2024selfcorrection, kamoi2024selfcorrection}.

Recent self-correction benchmarks sharpen this concern by distinguishing solution production from solution review. The self-correction blind spot describes cases where models fail to identify mistakes in outputs attributed to themselves but can detect similar errors in external outputs \citep{tsui2025blindspot}. This literature motivates our initial identity-swap design. However, our data suggest that in a correction-oriented JSON critique interface, the larger and more stable failure mode is not identity-specific blindness but over-acceptance of wrong solutions across source labels.

\subsection{LLM-as-Judge Calibration and Reliability}
\label{sec:rw-judge}

LLM-as-judge systems use models to evaluate candidate outputs, often replacing or augmenting human evaluation. Such judges can be useful, but their reliability depends on calibration, prompt design, and the match between the judgment task and the model's capabilities. Zheng et al.\ show that LLM judges can reproduce some human preferences in chat evaluation while remaining sensitive to position, verbosity, and presentation effects \citep{zheng2024judging}. Prior work already shows that LLM confidence can be miscalibrated and that elicitation strategy affects verbalized confidence \citep{tian2023justask}. Critique benchmarks similarly show that stronger models tend to be better critics, but critique quality remains far from solved on reasoning tasks \citep{lin2024criticbench}.

Our work does not claim overconfidence itself as a new phenomenon. Instead, we ask whether the same prompt-interface choices that change verdict behavior also change the calibration of the critic's reported confidence. A high false-accept rate means the critic approves incorrect reasoning and prevents downstream correction from even being attempted. This is distinct from correction quality conditional on rejection. A repair agent can only fix an error if the verdict stage first declares that the solution is flawed. The results therefore report verdict metrics separately from repair metrics whenever possible.

\subsection{Self-Preference and Authorship Attribution}
\label{sec:rw-self-preference}

Self-preference work shows that LLM evaluators can favor their own generations or generations attributed to their own identity. Panickssery et al.\ find that LLM evaluators recognize and favor their own outputs, while Wataoka et al.\ relate self-preference bias to distributional familiarity in LLM-as-judge settings \citep{panickssery2024selfpreference, wataoka2024selfpreference}. Lehr et al.\ further argue that assigned identity can be sufficient to induce strong preference shifts, indicating that the label in the prompt can matter even when the underlying content is unchanged \citep{lehr2025extremeselfpreference}. Xu et al.\ report that self-bias can be amplified during self-refinement \citep{xu2024pride}.

These findings provided the motivation for our identity conditions, but our empirical result differs from the strongest version of this prior expectation. In the present correction-oriented setup, ordinary identity labels produce small changes in false acceptance relative to the baseline over-acceptance rate. The difference does not imply that self-preference findings are wrong; it indicates that authorship attribution effects depend on the evaluation protocol, task, and output format. In our setting, interface design dominates identity framing.

\subsection{Prompt-Format and Structured-Output Sensitivity}
\label{sec:rw-prompt-format}

LLM behavior is sensitive to prompt wording and response format. Source framing experiments demonstrate that identical content can receive different evaluations when attributed to different sources \citep{germani2025sourceframing}. Structured-output prompts introduce another layer of sensitivity because the requested fields define what the model believes the task is. A JSON schema that asks only for a verdict may elicit a different policy from one that asks for a verdict plus a corrected answer, even if both prompts begin with the same instruction to review the solution carefully.

This is the key methodological concern addressed by the Caveat A ablation. The first two-stage diagnostic used a verdict-only prompt that explicitly stated not to produce a corrected answer. That instruction may itself have increased skepticism. The new ablation therefore separates three factors: the absence of a repair field, the explicit no-repair instruction, and the presence of a schema slot for a corrected answer.

\subsection{Critique-Repair Pipelines}
\label{sec:rw-pipelines}

Many self-improvement systems use a critique-repair pipeline: generate an answer, ask a model to critique it, and ask for a revision. Multi-agent debate and cross-model review methods extend this idea by using other models or agents to challenge an initial answer \citep{du2023debate, liang2023encouraging}. Cross-context review also suggests that separating production and review can improve output quality by reducing anchoring to the original generation process \citep{song2026crosscontext}.

Our contribution is narrower than multi-agent debate and simpler than model ensembles. We examine the interface inside a single critic call. A system can ask for a verdict first and a repair second, or it can ask for both at once. The distinction is small in implementation but large enough to change the measured false-accept rate. This makes critique-repair pipeline design a reliability issue, not merely a formatting preference.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{Task Setting}
\label{sec:task-setting}

Each experimental unit consists of a problem, a candidate solution, a ground-truth answer, a critic model, a source-framing condition, and a prompt-interface variant. The candidate solution includes the solver's reasoning trace and final answer. The critic receives the problem and solution and returns a structured assessment. The primary verdict outcome is whether the critic marks the solution as correct. For actually wrong solutions, marking the solution as correct is a false accept. For actually correct solutions, marking it as wrong is a false rejection.

The distinction between wrong and correct candidate solutions is determined before critique using dataset-specific answer checking. This design lets us measure the critic's verdict independently of whether its proposed repair is ultimately correct. When a repair is requested and supplied, we also evaluate whether the corrected answer matches the ground truth.

\subsection{Datasets}
\label{sec:datasets}

Experiments use four reasoning datasets that differ in domain and answer format. AIME contains competition mathematics problems with short numeric answers. GPQA contains graduate-level scientific multiple-choice questions designed to require expert knowledge \citep{rein2023gpqa}. MMLU-Pro is used as a broad multiple-choice reasoning benchmark. Olympiad contains challenging mathematical problems. Earlier exploratory runs included BBH, but it was dropped from the main analysis because it was too easy for the present critique setup and did not stress the verdict stage enough.

The existing full sweeps cover AIME, GPQA, MMLU-Pro, and Olympiad. The Caveat A prompt-interface ablation samples from these same datasets using seed 42: candidate identifiers are shuffled once, then the first 100 actually wrong solutions and first 50 actually correct solutions are selected per dataset, using all available items when a dataset has fewer than the target. The cached sample identifiers are stored under \texttt{logs/caveat\_a\_samples/}. This sampling preserves paired comparisons across prompt variants while keeping the ablation small enough to run quickly.

\subsection{Solver and Critic Models}
\label{sec:models}

The experiments include five critic configurations: qwen3, qwen3-no-think, gpt-oss-think, gpt-oss-no-think, and Gemma-4. The qwen3 and gpt-oss variants include thinking and no-thinking configurations, which are analyzed separately because their critique behavior differs. The main two-stage verdict-repair diagnostic was completed for qwen3-no-think and gpt-oss-no-think. The four-arm Caveat A ablation was completed for qwen3-no-think, gpt-oss-no-think, and Gemma-4. Gemma-4 is used as a cross-critic evaluating gpt-oss-think solutions, while the two no-thinking critics evaluate their own model-family solution pools.

All main critics are served through the repository's model presets. qwen3-no-think uses \texttt{Qwen/Qwen3-32B} through vLLM with \texttt{enable\_thinking=False}; gpt-oss-no-think uses \texttt{openai/gpt-oss-20b} through vLLM with \texttt{reasoning\_effort=low}; Gemma-4 uses \texttt{google/gemma-4-31B-it} through vLLM. For all primary results in this paper (the Caveat A four-arm ablation, the field-order ablation, the gated mitigation pipeline, the N-of-K majority baseline, and the cross-critique replication), the critic experiment runners override the preset sampling parameters with \texttt{temperature=0.0} (deterministic decoding). The preset's default sampling parameters (\texttt{temperature=0.7} with \texttt{top\_p=0.8} and \texttt{top\_k=20} for qwen3-no-think; \texttt{temperature=0.7} for gpt-oss-no-think) were used only for some early legacy identity-swap sweeps; that data appears in this paper exclusively as legacy baseline context (Table~\ref{tab:baseline-far} and the broader source-framing summary), not in any prompt-interface contrast. Critic \texttt{max\_tokens} is set to 40000 for the local Qwen/gpt-oss critics and 12000 for Gemma-4. The default vLLM endpoint is \texttt{http://localhost:8000/v1}.

Candidate solutions are generated once and then reused across source-framing and prompt-interface conditions. This paired design ensures that changes in verdict behavior are attributable to the critic prompt or framing rather than to different underlying solution content.

\subsection{Source-Framing Conditions}
\label{sec:identity-conditions}

The source-framing experiment presents the same solution under six labels. The \textsc{Self} condition describes the solution as the critic's previous solution. The \textsc{Other Model} condition describes it as generated by another AI model. The \textsc{Anonymous} condition gives no source information. The \textsc{Human} condition attributes the solution to a human student. The \textsc{Weak Model} condition attributes it to a weaker, less capable AI model. The \textsc{Strong Model} condition attributes it to a stronger model.

The first four labels test ordinary authorship attribution. The weak and strong labels test whether source competence cues shift the critic's prior toward skepticism or deference. The pre-registered competence-axis prediction was that \textsc{Weak Model} would decrease false acceptance and \textsc{Strong Model} would increase false acceptance. Existing data support the first half but not the symmetric mirror pattern: \textsc{Weak Model} reduces false acceptance across critics, while \textsc{Strong Model} is near null for Gemma and gpt-oss-no-think and only mildly positive for qwen3-no-think.

\subsection{Prompt-Interface Variants}
\label{sec:prompt-variants}

The central procedural variable is the critic's output contract. The standard verdict-plus-repair prompt asks the critic to decide whether the solution is correct, describe the error if any, provide a corrected answer, and report confidence. The initial verdict-only diagnostic removed the corrected-answer field and instructed the model not to produce a correction before giving the verdict.

The Caveat A ablation expands this into four prompt-interface variants. The \textsc{Verdict-Only Instructed} variant is the original verdict-only prompt, including the explicit instruction not to produce a corrected answer. The \textsc{Verdict-Only Neutral} variant asks for the same three fields but removes the explicit no-repair instruction. The \textsc{Schema-Matched Verdict} variant uses the neutral verdict-only instructions but includes a fourth field, \texttt{corrected\_answer}, fixed to null, so that the response schema has the same number of fields as the repair prompt without requesting repair. The \textsc{Verdict+Repair} variant asks for the corrected final answer when the solution is judged wrong. Follow-up field-order ablations for gpt-oss-no-think and Gemma-4 add \textsc{Verdict+Repair Reversed}, which asks for \texttt{corrected\_answer} before \texttt{is\_correct}; this tests whether the verdict shift depends on schema-field order.

These four arms identify whether the measured verdict changes with the repair task itself, with the earlier no-repair instruction, or with structured-output schema differences. The primary comparison is \textsc{Verdict+Repair} versus \textsc{Verdict-Only Neutral}. Secondary comparisons are \textsc{Verdict-Only Instructed} versus \textsc{Verdict-Only Neutral} and \textsc{Schema-Matched Verdict} versus \textsc{Verdict-Only Neutral}.

\subsection{Two-Stage and Gated Critique Designs}
\label{sec:two-stage}

The two-stage diagnostic issues independent calls for verdict-only and verdict-plus-repair outputs. The calls share the same problem, solution, and source preamble, but they do not share conversational context. This independence avoids anchoring the repair call on the first verdict and lets us compare how the output contract changes first-pass judgment.

The mitigation experiment uses a gated design. The critic first produces a neutral verdict-only assessment. If the verdict accepts the solution, the pipeline stops and records acceptance. If the verdict rejects the solution, a second repair prompt asks for a corrected answer. The gated design tests the practical recommendation implied by the main result: log a verdict before asking for repair, and request repair only after rejection. It is evaluated against the standard one-shot verdict-plus-repair pipeline on the same sampled items.

\subsection{Logging Schema and Answer Checking}
\label{sec:logging}

Each critique record stores the problem identifier, dataset, solver model, critic model, source-framing condition, prompt-interface variant, parsed verdict, error description, corrected answer when applicable, confidence, raw response, token counts, and the precomputed correctness of the candidate solution. Ground-truth checking uses dataset-specific answer normalization. For short-answer mathematics datasets, the final answer is normalized before exact or symbolic comparison. For multiple-choice datasets, the selected option is compared against the answer key. Corrected answers are evaluated with the same checking function used for solver answers. The main verdict analyses do not depend on corrected-answer quality.

\subsection{Parse Handling and Parser-Aware Replay}
\label{sec:parse-handling}

The structured-output parser tries direct JSON parsing, fenced-block extraction, and a regex search for a JSON object containing \texttt{is\_correct}. If all three fail, the production parser returns a fallback value of \texttt{is\_correct=True} with \texttt{corrected\_answer=None} and \texttt{confidence=0.5}. This fallback is conservative for downstream safety because an unparseable response preserves the original candidate answer. It is permissive for false-accept-rate measurement on actually wrong items because an unparseable response is counted as an accept.

The revised analysis therefore replays the parser offline on stored \texttt{raw\_response} strings using \texttt{analysis/parser\_artifact\_audit.py}. This replay reconstructs per-arm parse-failure flags without rerunning model inference. We report verdict contrasts both with parse failures included, matching the original parsed-output metric, and with any pair containing a parse failure excluded. The paired exclusion is used only as a parser-aware sensitivity analysis; the included metric remains useful for measuring an end-to-end structured critic interface, where malformed output is itself a failure mode.

\subsection{Metrics}
\label{sec:metrics}

The primary metric is false-accept rate, defined on actually wrong candidate solutions as the fraction judged correct by the critic. Lower false-accept rate indicates better error detection. The corresponding metric on actually correct solutions is false-rejection rate, the fraction judged wrong by the critic. We also report detection accuracy over both correct and wrong solutions, correction success among rejected wrong solutions, harmful correction among correct solutions that are falsely rejected and then changed, and mean confidence. Calibration is reported as expected calibration error (ECE) using ten equal-width bins on stated confidence in $[0,1]$, computed per (critic, prompt-interface, dataset) and summarized both pooled and as an equal-dataset-weighted macro mean. Items that produced a parse failure at logging time are excluded from confidence-based metrics. As an end-to-end summary, we also report $P(\text{final correct}) = p \cdot (1 - \mathrm{HCR}) + (1-p) \cdot \mathrm{WRCR}$, where $p$ is the fraction of incoming solutions that are already correct, $\mathrm{HCR}$ is the harmful-correction rate on correct items, and $\mathrm{WRCR}$ is the repair-success rate on wrong items.

Repair metrics are interpreted separately from verdict metrics. A low false-accept rate is not sufficient if the critic rejects many correct solutions or generates poor repairs. Conversely, poor repair quality does not invalidate a verdict-stage finding; it indicates a different bottleneck in the critique-repair pipeline.

\subsection{Statistical Analysis}
\label{sec:stats}

All primary comparisons are item-paired. For primary false-accept differences, we report absolute percentage-point differences with paired bootstrap confidence intervals over problem identifiers. The primary analysis uses paired bootstrap intervals for the pre-specified prompt-interface contrasts. As a model-based robustness check, we fit per-critic generalized estimating equation (GEE) binomial logistic regressions with cluster-robust standard errors by problem identifier and \textsc{Verdict-Only Neutral} as the reference arm. We use GEE rather than a mixed-effects model in the final analysis because it gave stable cluster-robust estimates for every critic and prompt arm.

The primary prompt-interface diagnostic is \textsc{Verdict+Repair} versus \textsc{Verdict-Only Neutral}, reported both under the original parsed-output metric and under parser-aware exclusion. Secondary prompt contrasts examine the explicit no-repair instruction, the schema-matched control, the field-order arm, leave-one-dataset-out robustness, and cross-critique replication. Identity and source-framing contrasts use \textsc{Anonymous} as the reference condition and are treated as contextual analyses. We report both odds ratios and absolute percentage-point effects where model fits are used, with emphasis on absolute effects because the baseline false-accept rates are high. To control family-wise error on the per-critic Caveat A prompt contrasts (nine tests: three contrasts $\times$ three critics), we additionally report Holm--Bonferroni adjusted $p$-values estimated by paired bootstrap; results are summarized in Table~\ref{tab:holm}. Revision analyses that compare interface variance, parser-aware contrasts, thinking modes, and abstention thresholds are generated by \texttt{analysis/revision\_v4\_analyses.py}.


\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Over-Acceptance Is the Dominant Baseline Failure}
\label{sec:results-baseline}

Before considering source labels or repair requests, the critics already accept a large fraction of wrong solutions. In the legacy full-call sweeps, false-accept rates are 55.9\% for Gemma-4, 77.1\% for gpt-oss-no-think, and 56.9\% for qwen3-no-think. The neutral verdict-only ablation arms remain high: 46.7\%, 64.0\%, and 57.9\%, respectively. These values show that the main reliability problem is not a small identity gap but a broad failure to reject flawed reasoning.

\subsection{Interface Variation Exceeds Identity Variation}
\label{sec:results-interface-identity}

The central comparison is between ordinary identity-label variation and prompt-interface variation. Ordinary identity labels are small: across anonymous, human, other-model, and self labels, the four-label FAR range is 2.76 percentage points for Gemma-4, 0.81 points for gpt-oss-no-think, and 1.96 points for qwen3-no-think. The prompt-interface range over the Caveat A arms is larger for every critic with both measurements available: 8.43, 7.78, and 7.41 points, respectively. Thus, the interface range is 3.06--9.65 times the identity-label range (Table~\ref{tab:interface-identity}).

This is the paper's main positive result. It does not require a universal repair-request mechanism. The same solution content is being measured through different source labels and different output contracts, and the output contract moves the measurement more than ordinary authorship attribution. Low-competence framing still matters as an asymmetric skepticism cue, but it is secondary to the broader prompt-interface brittleness.

This diagnostic showed that the critique interface mattered, but it did not identify the mechanism.

Parser-aware replay changes the interpretation of the Caveat A repair contrast. The verdict-plus-repair prompt produces more malformed structured outputs than the neutral verdict-only prompt. Because the production fallback maps parse failure to \texttt{is\_correct=True}, parse failures on wrong items become false accepts under the included parsed-output metric. When pairs containing a parse failure are excluded, the apparent Gemma-4 repair effect collapses from +4.52 points to -0.34 points, and the gpt-oss-no-think contrast weakens from +4.61 points to +1.42 points with a confidence interval crossing zero. qwen3-no-think remains negative under both analyses. Per-dataset replay shows the same pattern: Gemma-4 AIME moves from +21.9 points to 0.0, Gemma-4 Olympiad moves from +8.0 to 0.0, and gpt-oss-no-think AIME is reduced from +23.4 to +12.0.

The correct conclusion is therefore not that verdict-plus-repair robustly increases false acceptance as a behavioral verdict effect. The correct conclusion is that repair-coupled structured prompts are measurement-unsafe unless parse failures are explicitly logged and analyzed. The included metric remains relevant for end-to-end system behavior because malformed output is a real interface failure; the excluded metric is necessary for isolating behavioral verdict changes from parser artifacts.

\subsection{Instruction and Schema Perturbations Still Shift Verdicts}
\label{sec:results-caveat-a}

\subsection{Schema-Order Sensitivity Is Critic-Dependent}
\label{sec:results-field-order}

The field-order ablation asks whether schema layout itself is an interface perturbation. In the standard \textsc{Verdict+Repair} arm, the model emits \texttt{is\_correct} before generating \texttt{corrected\_answer}. In the reversed arm, the prompt asks for \texttt{corrected\_answer} before \texttt{is\_correct}. This manipulation changes the structured response channel and the measured verdict, but not in a universal direction.

For gpt-oss-no-think, the answer-first arm returns the included parsed-output contrast close to the neutral/schema-matched baseline. For Gemma-4, the answer-first arm increases the included FAR even more than the standard repair arm. Given the parser-aware repair finding, this result should not be read as a single generation-order mechanism. It is evidence that field order is itself part of the measurement interface and can interact with model behavior and structured-output reliability differently across critics.

\subsection{Mitigation Requires Separating Measurement From Intervention}
\label{sec:results-mitigation}

The most robust mitigation is not a new repair prompt but a measurement protocol: log a neutral verdict and parse status before requesting repair. The gated pipeline implements this protocol by first collecting a neutral verdict-only assessment and requesting repair only after rejection. Under the included parsed-output metric, gating lowers one-shot FAR for Gemma-4 and gpt-oss-no-think and is small for qwen3-no-think, consistent with the broader interface-sensitivity pattern. N-of-K majority voting requires five calls and does not dominate the one-call gated verdict stage. Confidence-based abstention is also not a clean fix: for qwen3-no-think under the neutral verdict-only arm, increasing the confidence threshold from 0.5 to 0.95 lowers coverage from 74.7\% to 52.1\%, but FAR among retained predictions rises from 68.7\% to 95.1\%. \subsection{Repair Quality and End-to-End Utility Remain Poor}
\label{sec:results-repair-quality}

Separating verdict from repair is necessary because repair quality is weak even when the critic correctly detects an error. Among wrong solutions that are correctly rejected, repair success is only 10--22\% for the main no-thinking and Gemma critics. Thinking improves repair quality, but even the better gpt-oss-think legacy configuration leaves most detected errors unrepaired. The other side of the tradeoff is harmful correction: when a critic falsely rejects a correct solution, it often replaces the correct answer with a wrong one.

End-to-end utility makes the same point. At an incoming correctness rate of $p=0.5$, the pipeline is slightly positive for Gemma-4 but negative for gpt-oss-no-think and qwen3-no-think. Repair is therefore not a guaranteed benefit that can compensate for brittle verdict measurement. For critics whose downstream repair quality is poor, the more reliable use of the verdict is triage, with repair delegated to a different model or a human.

\subsection{Prompt Interface Shifts Calibration}
\label{sec:results-calibration}

A natural fallback is to ask whether the confidence field identifies unreliable verdicts. It does not solve the interface problem, because calibration itself shifts with the prompt interface. Absolute LLM overconfidence is already well documented, so the calibration result here is not that critics are overconfident in general. The interface-specific result is that calibration changes when the verdict interface changes. Gemma-4's ECE moves from 0.258 in the legacy full-call prompt to 0.314 in the verdict-only neutral prompt and 0.290 in the verdict-plus-repair prompt. gpt-oss-no-think is consistently poorly calibrated, moving from 0.389 to 0.395 and 0.404. The largest shift is qwen3-no-think: ECE worsens from 0.275 in the legacy setting to 0.463 under the verdict-only neutral interface and remains high at 0.416 under verdict-plus-repair. Equal-dataset macro ECE shows the same pattern, moving from 0.263 to 0.460 and 0.419, so the shift is not a pooled-data weighting artifact.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{What the Paper Shows}
\label{sec:discussion-shows}

The main result is that LLM critic verdicts are interface-contingent measurements. In this correction-oriented setup, ordinary identity labels produce small shifts, while prompt-interface perturbations change verdict outcomes, parse reliability, calibration, and downstream repair behavior. The repair-coupled prompt was the motivating case, but parser-aware replay shows that the strongest apparent repair effect is artifact-dominated. The broader finding is more general: a single structured critic call should not be treated as a stable measurement of model judgment unless the output contract and parser behavior are part of the measurement.

\subsection{Why the Parser Finding Matters}
\label{sec:discussion-parser}

The parser finding is not a nuisance detail. It is a substantive measurement result. A structured-output critic can fail in two ways at once: it can make the wrong judgment, or it can produce an output that the evaluation pipeline maps into a judgment. When the fallback maps malformed responses to acceptance, parse failures on wrong items mechanically increase FAR. This does not make the interface harmless; it means the interface is even less suitable as a clean verdict channel. The included parsed-output metric measures the deployed structured interface, while parser-aware exclusion helps isolate behavioral verdict changes.

\subsection{Practical Recommendations}
\label{sec:discussion-practical}

The practical recommendation is to separate measurement from intervention. A critique pipeline should first elicit a neutral verdict-only response, persist parse status, and only then request repair if the verdict rejects the candidate solution. Reports should separate false acceptance, false rejection, parse failure, repair success, harmful correction, and calibration. Confidence thresholds should be reported with coverage, because abstention can concentrate high-confidence false accepts rather than remove them. N-of-K voting can be useful as an ensemble method, but it costs more calls and does not replace the need for a clean first-stage verdict.

\subsection{Relationship to Identity-Bias Work}
\label{sec:discussion-identity}

The identity-swap results should be read as a scoped negative result. Prior work shows that LLMs can exhibit self-preference and source-framing biases in evaluation settings \citep{panickssery2024selfpreference, lehr2025extremeselfpreference, germani2025sourceframing}. Our data show that those effects do not dominate in this correction-oriented protocol. The same solution presented as self, anonymous, human, or another model produces only small shifts in false acceptance compared with the shifts produced by interface perturbations. This difference may arise because correction-oriented critique requires error detection rather than preference ranking, because the JSON schema constrains the response, or because the datasets expose a broad over-acceptance failure that swamps identity labels.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

First, the results are critic- and dataset-dependent. The paper no longer claims a universal behavioral effect of repair requests. Instead, it claims interface brittleness, supported by interface-versus-identity variation, parser-aware replay, instruction and schema effects, field-order sensitivity, calibration shifts, and repair-utility results. The exact magnitude and direction of each perturbation depends on the critic and dataset.

Second, parser-aware replay is reconstructed offline from stored raw responses rather than from a parser flag persisted at generation time. This is sufficient to audit the main artifact because the raw response is available, but future experiments should persist parse status directly in the logging schema. The production fallback remains a design choice: it is conservative for preserving candidate answers but permissive for FAR on wrong items.

Third, the results are prompt-specific. The analysis covers several prompt-interface variants, including no-repair instruction, schema-matched verdicts, verdict-plus-repair, and field-order reversal, but other templates may reduce or amplify the same failure modes. This is not a reason to ignore the result; it is the reason the result matters. Interface choices that appear minor can change both verdict behavior and structured-output reliability.

Fourth, the experiments focus on reasoning benchmarks with checkable answers. This makes false acceptance and correction success measurable, but it may not generalize to open-ended writing, summarization, or subjective preference evaluation. In open-ended domains, the boundary between verdict and repair may be less crisp, and human evaluation may be needed to assess whether a repair improved the output.

Fifth, thinking-enabled critics are analyzed through legacy full-call data rather than a full parser-aware Caveat A sweep. The existing data answer the reviewer-facing question that thinking does not automatically reduce over-acceptance, while also showing that thinking can improve repair quality. A fully matched thinking-enabled prompt-interface ablation remains useful follow-up work.

Sixth, some legacy source-framing runs were collected across multiple log subdirectories and solver configurations. The main prompt-interface ablation uses locked sample identifiers and paired prompt variants to avoid this issue. Legacy identity and thinking-mode results are used primarily as contextual evidence rather than as the sole basis for the central claim.

Finally, answer-checking functions are imperfect. Mathematical equivalence, multiple-choice extraction, and boxed-answer parsing can introduce noise. Because the core comparisons are paired across prompt variants, such noise should affect paired arms similarly, but absolute rates should still be interpreted with this measurement layer in mind.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

LLM critique reliability cannot be read directly from a single structured prompt. In our experiments, ordinary identity labels produce small shifts, while prompt-interface perturbations alter verdicts, parse reliability, calibration, and repair outcomes. The apparent repair-request effect that motivated the study is itself a cautionary example: once parse failures are audited, the strongest false-acceptance contrast is artifact-dominated. The surviving lesson is broader and more practical. Critique systems should separate measurement from intervention: first elicit and log a neutral verdict with parse status, then request repair only as a downstream action, and evaluate repair success and harmful correction separately.
